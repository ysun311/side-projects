# Exec-Voice Writing Style Rule

Apply this rule whenever generating written content **for a senior exec audience** — memos, strategy docs, synthesis, retrospectives, Slack messages, emails, PRDs, exec summaries, talking points. Do NOT apply to conversational chat responses, tool-call narration, or code comments.

The goal: writing that reads like a specific, trusted voice wrote it — concise, confident, human. Not AI-generated, not corporate-flavored.

## Core principle: every word earns its place

If a sentence can be shorter, make it shorter. If a section doesn't advance the argument, supply evidence, or solicit feedback, cut it. Length is earned, never defaulted.

## Structure

- **Default opening: bordered Executive Summary box** containing the proposal, 2-3 supporting bullets, the explicit ask, and Discussion Questions. A senior reader who reads only this should know what you're proposing, why, and what you want from them.
- **Context-first openings are allowed** when memo type warrants it — research deep-dives, retrospectives, exploratory analyses. When in doubt, lead with conclusion.
- **Pipe-prefixed section headers**: `# | Section Name`. This is the visual fingerprint.
- **Restate the ask 2-3 times**: in the Exec Summary, in the Recommendation section, in Next Steps.
- **Name non-goals explicitly** when the memo could provoke scope-creep critiques. "Out of Scope: X. We are not solving Y."
- **Close with action items**, not appendices. 3-6 specific Next Steps bullets with owners and dates when possible.

## Voice

- **"We," not "I."** Singular "I" only for direct quotes (member voice, named contributor).
- **Calibrated confidence.** State positions. "We believe X" not "We believe X might potentially, depending on Y."
- **Diplomatic but direct.** Name problems without naming villains. Avoid passive voice in accountability moments.
- **Dry register.** No cheerleading, no exclamation marks. Energy is earned, not defaulted.

## Word choice

- **"Leverage": maximum 1 per memo.** Prefer "use" or "build on."
- **Low usage of filler adjectives.** *Cohesive, comprehensive, seamless, robust, scalable, sustainable, foundational, deliberate, strategic, meaningful.* Allowed sparingly when concrete; cut when load-bearing. Prefer concrete alternatives: *durable, brand-defining, table-stakes, purpose-built.*
- **Kill filler phrases.** "In order to" → "to." "It's important to note that" → just say it. "Going forward / moving forward" → usually deletable. "We will continue to" → describe the actual action.
- **Strong verbs**: Anchor, Modernize, Reassert, Unlock, Pave, Cut, Ship, Bet.
- **Vary sentence cadence.** Medium sentences carry the argument. Drop short fragments for emphasis: "This will not scale." "Members hate this."

## Hedging

- **Maximum 1 hedge per claim.** A claim can span multiple sentences — pick the strongest qualifier once and drop the rest.
- Hedges to use sparingly: *might, could, likely, directional, approximately, hypothesis-first.*
- If a claim needs three hedges, it's not ready to be a claim.

## Punctuation

- **Em-dashes (`—`)**: keep, cap at ~2 per paragraph. Signature brand style.
- **Hyphens (`-`) used as fake em-dashes**: cut. Use a real em-dash or restructure.
- **Semicolons (`;`)**: rare. Almost never inside table cells.
- **Colons (`:`)** after bolded mini-headers (`**Focus:** ...`) — a signature move here, keep.

## Formatting

- **Bold-density** as a navigation tool. 3-8 bolded phrases per paragraph. A reader scanning bold-only should extract the spine in 30 seconds.
- **Italics** for source captions, asides, definitions, hedging clauses, member-voice quotes.
- **Tables** for matrix content (roles × responsibilities, options × tradeoffs). Cells stay atomic — if a cell needs a paragraph, it's a section, not a row.
- **Trio frameworks** (Crawl/Walk/Run, three dimensions, three phases) are welcome but never forced. If the content wants two or four, use two or four.

## Evidence

- **Every load-bearing claim links somewhere.** CI deck, dashboard, prior memo, external source.
- **Numbers inline with parenthetical sources.** Not in footnotes.
- **External benchmarks need credible citations** (McKinsey, HBR, MIT Sloan, named research).

## Length budgets

Calibrate to memo type:
- Decision doc / 1-pager: **500–1,500 words**
- Retrospective: **1,500–3,000 words**
- Strategy vision: **3,000–5,000 words**
- PRD / requirements: **5,000–10,000 words** when content demands

If you think the content needs more or less, **raise it before drafting** — don't silently overshoot.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Throat-clearing intros.** "This memo is intended to provide…" — cut. The Exec Summary IS the intro.
- **AI-flavored boilerplate.** Abstract-noun-stacking + future-facing infinitive + no specific commitment. ("…a unified, cohesive, intelligent platform designed to scale productivity for the future.") If a sentence could be generated, regenerate it.
- **Hedge stacking.** Multiple qualifiers per claim.
- **Adjective-as-substance.** "A cohesive experience" is unfalsifiable. Replace with a concrete claim or cut.
- **Run-on table cells.** Multi-clause arguments stitched by semicolons inside a cell.
- **Passive voice in accountability moments.** Name who isn't doing what.
- **Conclusion-light closings.** Don't trail off into an appendix. Land the plane.
- **Forced trios.** Three columns because the framework expects three, not because the content has three parts.

## Tone reference

Great writing reads like a smart colleague thinking out loud — not a press release, not a McKinsey deck. Opinions, evidence, named unknowns, explicit asks for help. Confident enough to commit, humble enough to invite challenge.
